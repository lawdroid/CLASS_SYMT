The §3.11 geometry lesson

Three mock posteriors with similar indirect signals (same ρ(median)/ρ(peak) range, similar R−1 plateau character, similar best-fit vs median drift) but completely different real geometry. The lesson: look at the 2D KDE before telling a story. ← back to summary

What the indirect signals can and can't tell you

The first three rows can't tell A from B. Only the KDE (the bottom row) actually separates them. Two of these three posteriors are unimodal; one is bimodal. From the indirect signals alone, you cannot tell which is which.

The 3b "two clean basins" reconstruction from 2026-05-12 to 2026-05-15 was a version of this trap. The indirect signals all flashed bimodality, but the real KDE showed shape A: skewed unimodal. The model implied by those indirect signals was geometrically wrong, and the rest of the analysis built on it had to be unwound. The cost was ~3 days. Every future Phase has to pull the KDE first.

The rule, codified for next time

  1. Before describing posterior geometry in writing or to a collaborator, pull the actual 2D KDE for the relevant parameter pair from the converged chain. Use getdist, cobaya-post, or the chains_summary.json exporter, but always start with the KDE.
  2. Treat ρ(median)/ρ(peak) < 0.5 as "median is in low-density territory; the geometry needs investigation," not as "bimodal." Same for R−1 plateau character and best-fit drift.
  3. Quote percentiles of a flat 1D marginal as descriptive statistics of the prior, not as data-driven bounds. (β's flat 3b marginal is the example: q16 = 0.34 does not mean "β > 0.34 ruled in"; it means 16% across a uniform range.)
